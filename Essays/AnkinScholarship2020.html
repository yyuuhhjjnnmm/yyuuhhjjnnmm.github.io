INTRODUCTION

Ever since 2009, when Uber came on to the scene, the world has embraced ride hailing apps (RHA). While young RHA startups like Uber and Lyft grew, they captured market share by addressing the existing issues of taking taxis, which helped them explode into popularity. However, as their business grew, a nasty side effect of the fast, loosely regulated expansion came to light. Week after week, it seemed that reports of scandalous incidents ranging from verbal harassment and physical assault to rape and even murder became a regular occurence.

In Uber's first safety report released in November 2019, Uber reported about three thousand sexual assaults out of their 1.3 billions rides in the USA in 2018. The New York Times (NYT) compared that to the NYPD's report of 533 sex crimes on the NY transit system, out of 1.6 billion rides in 2018. The NYT article drew a stark contrast between the two transportation platforms with similar riderships. (https://www.nytimes.com/2019/12/05/technology/uber-sexual-assaults-murders-deaths-safety.html) Although public transit has a different dynamic than taking a private ride, 6 times the number of sexual assault is unacceptable.

Given the numbers, it is clear that RHA are not doing enough to protect their passengers. In this essay, I suggest three areas of improvement:

  1. their pre-screening procedure, as a preventative measure
  2. their in-ride safety feature, to help safety during rides, and
  3. their handling of complaints, after an incident has occurred

These three points cover the entire timeline of a user's experience, from before the ride, during the ride and after the incident after a ride. This multifaceted approach should help RHA promote safety by addressing and preventing more occurrences of criminal incidents during rides.

BEFORE RIDES: IMPROVE SCREENING

Over the past few years, RHA such as Uber and Lyft have been at the forefront of scandalous incidents. A few recent examples include:

  • a sexual assault incident of a female Los Angeles passanger in 2017
  • a sexual assault by a Seattle Uber driver in 2018, and
  • a kidnapping and rape of a female passanger by a New Orleans Lyft driver in 2019

(https://ankinlaw.com/rideshare-services-safety/)

It is clear that there is a subset of RHA drivers who have criminal intent, and the RHA companies need to do a better job preventing them from exploiting their platforms.


        In Massachusetts, a 2016 law requires RHA drivers to be further screened by the state before being able to drive passengers. In an article by WHDH, a local Boston Massachusetts news station, it is reported that the Massachusetts Department of Public Utilities rejected nearly 16% of applicants that passed the initial background checks by the RHA companies in 2018. Out of the rejected applicants, "more than 5,000 applicants were rejected due to a past violent crime or because they are registered sex offenders".

(https://whdh.com/news/mass-dept-of-public-utilities-more-than-30000-ride-sharing-applicants-rejected-in-2018/) According to the Ankin Law Office, it is clear that "... Uber and Lyft routinely fail to perform adequate background checks on drivers and fail to monitor drivers for inappropriate conduct after they are hired". (https://ankinlaw.com/rideshare-services-safety/)

Since 2018, RHA have committed to more stringent background checks, including criminal, driving and sexual registry, and the industry wide standard of annual rescreening. However, it is clear that these companies need to continue to improve on their hiring practice.

Connecticut Democrat Senator Richard Blumenthal was quoted saying that “... these ride-hailing companies have been abjectly failing in their duty to protect against predators or criminals”. The senator has also been an advocate of more stringent background checks, including fingerprint based biometric background checks (https://www.nytimes.com/2019/12/05/technology/uber-sexual-assaults-murders-deaths-safety.html), which is a sentiment shared by other law officials as well. (https://ankinlaw.com/rideshare-services-safety/)

It is clear that RHA companies need to take a more serious approach when it comes to screening, with stricter background checks. Furthermore, they also need to consistently reassess drivers, possibly more than the current industry standard of annual re-screening. Screening is the most straightforward way to prevent predators and criminals from entering the platform and endangering passengers, and the onus lies with the RHA companies to pick up this slack.

DURING RIDES: RECORDINGS

Over the past few years, in order to address passenger safety, the RHA have been pushing out more safety features. Reminding passengers to check the "vehicle’s license plate, make and model, and the name and photo ID of the driver before getting into the car". (https://ankinlaw.com/uber-alert-system/) Alongside that, more advanced features that have been include:

  • in app 911 call with location sharing to dispatchers(https://www.uber.com/ca/en/safety/)
  • predictive help by checking up on passengers (when going off route, stopping too soon, unusual duration)
  • live location and tracking with family and friends. (https://www.lyft.com/safety).

However, given the ongoing and continuous reports of incidents, it seems as if these measures are not enough.

After facing criticism about rider safety, the Chinese RHA Didi implemented audio recording of rides in September 2018. The audio recordings were mandatory, encrypted, and would get deleted after seven days if there were no complaints. The company has stood by this measure, proposing it as a deterrent for crimes during RHA rides due to surveillance and the resulting evidence. (https://www.scmp.com/tech/apps-social/article/2163488/chinas-didi-chuxing-rolls-out-car-audio-recording-part-safety)

In November 2019, the Washington Post reported that Uber also announced an audio recording protocol. Uber will first pilot audio recording in smaller markets, and later will roll-out the feature to the US after successful testing. The article also described the backlash the company has received over this, as privacy concerns about illegal wiretap, eavesdropping and privacy concerns have been brought up. (https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2019/11/20/uber-plans-start-audio-recording-rides-us-safety/ )

Even though privacy rights and consent laws are important, and the trade off between safety and customer privacy rights might seem like an impossible decision, however the safety of riders should prevail if these recording features do prevent further criminal incidents. Michael Bomberger, the San Diego-based lawyer who filed the lawsuit on behalf of 19 women against Lyft, "said the company needed to do far more, like installing video and audio surveillance that would let drivers know they are being recorded."(https://www.nytimes.com/2019/12/05/business/lyft-sexual-assault-lawsuit.html )

AFTER INCIDENT: COOPERATE WITH LAW ENFORCEMENT

After all the pre-emptive procedures and in-ride safety features, there are still bound to be incidents. When these inevitable incidents seep through the crack, RHA need to support criminal investigations instead of impeding it.

In the 19 women lawsuit against Lyft, "the women allege that Lyft 'chooses to stonewall' law enforcement investigating assaults." (https://www.cnn.com/2019/12/04/tech/lyft-sexual-assault-lawsuit/index.html)

According to a Reuters article studying the 2019 Uber safety report, a similar sentiment towards law enforcement can be found with Uber. From the article, Reuters explained that "Uber … received nearly 22,000 requests from U.S. law enforcement for user account details in 2018 and produced at least some data in 72% of the cases." The extent of "at least some data" is not clear, and the fact that in 28% of cases Uber failed to provide any data is jaw-dropping. It is evident that RHA companies are reluctant to fully cooperate with police when it comes to releasing private information. (https://www.reuters.com/article/us-uber-safety-drivers-focus/ubers-challenge-to-balance-driver-safety-with-customer-privacy-idUSKBN20X187)

In defense of the RHA companies, they need to protect sensitive user data. Here, we are yet again faced with the balance of safety and privacy, similar to the question of audio recording. Although individual privacy rights are crucial and a foundational part of basic rights, the police cannot conduct proper investigations when the RHA are withholding critical information about the alleged assailant. The RHA companies need to cooperate better with police departments and find a suitable middleground, in order to ensure safety and that offenders are properly charged.

CONCLUSION

It is clear that RHA have come a long way since their start in the early 2010s. With greater awareness from the general public, these companies have faced pressure to improve safety for the users of their apps. These external pressures have resulted in stricter and more frequency background checkers, better in-app safety features, public education campaigns, and more. The Uber 2019 Safety Report was a groundbreaking step forward, leading the industry in transparency and could be seen as a genuine step towards making a safer platform.

As Jeanne Christensen, a lawyer of rape victims during Uber rides, said: “the more that the public is aware, the more the company and everyone else has to respond”. These responses need to put safety as priority because “it’s [RHA] such a part of daily life that everyone is going to take it. We’re already at that point. So now they just have to make it as safe as possible.” (Jeanne Christensen) (https://www.nytimes.com/2019/12/05/technology/uber-sexual-assaults-murders-deaths-safety.html)
